// the book — six chapter ideas

Tres millones de viviendas

How to move from scarcity to abundance · Debate (Penguin Random House), September 2025

Spain's housing crisis is the central case study. But the underlying story — a generation locked out, a political economy that defends the status quo, and a public conversation that mistakes managing scarcity for solving it — is not unique to Spain. The book opens with a sentence I heard a 28-year-old say on the metro: "you're not going to have a house in your fucking life." The book is an answer to that. Six chapters, in two parts: why scarcity exists, and what to do about it.

  1. 01

    Prices rise because flats are scarce.

    The first chapter establishes the diagnosis. Spanish rents and prices haven't risen because of a credit bubble (this isn't 2007). They've risen because the number of households is growing — driven by net immigration and shrinking household size — while the construction of new housing keeps falling further behind. Tourist and seasonal demand on top. The rental market becomes a trap: short contracts, scarce supply, prices that compound. The numbers are concrete and brutal.

  2. 02

    Buying a home is a zero-sum game.

    Today's market is overheating without the cheap-credit dynamic of the previous decade. So who's keeping the few flats that come up for sale? The surprising answer: the urban middle class above 50, with savings, accumulated equity, and the ability to add a second or third property. New entrants — the under-35s without inherited capital — are systematically outbid. The fairness story is generational before it's anything else.

  3. 03

    Why the market doesn't respond.

    If demand is so visible, why doesn't supply catch up? Cheap credit is gone (rightly so). Construction productivity has stagnated for two decades. Land where people actually want to live is locked away by a regulatory and zoning system written for a different era. The cost structure of building has become hostile to anything but luxury. Risk and uncertainty drive professional investors elsewhere. None of this is a mystery — it's been documented. It just hasn't been acted on.

  4. 04

    The state doesn't pick up the slack either.

    Spain's public housing stock is roughly 2.5% of the total — one of the lowest in Europe. The post-2008 austerity decade hollowed out public construction capacity. Local authorities, who hold the keys to land and zoning, face incentives that point against new development: existing residents vote, future residents don't. The 2023 housing law is a meaningful intervention but mostly on the demand side; on supply it changes little. Public action has been timid where it most needed to be ambitious.

  5. 05

    Unlocking housing.

    The book's pivot point. Three million new homes over a decade is the rough order of magnitude Spain needs. Where: in the cities and metropolitan areas where economic opportunity already concentrates, not in the empty interior. How: a coordinated mix of density bonuses, large-scale public land mobilization, zoning reform with national criteria over local veto, public–private partnerships with skin-in-the-game risk-sharing, and a serious public construction effort. Three million is ambitious but not maximalist. It's what the demographics demand.

  6. 06

    Policies for access.

    Supply alone isn't a complete answer. The closing chapter takes the demand-side instruments — income transfers, rental aid, targeted public housing — and asks how to design them so they don't make scarcity worse. Many current policies (rent caps in shortage markets, blanket subsidies without supply expansion) push prices in the wrong direction. The chapter argues for sequencing: build first, support second; concentrate help on first-time entrants; tie public investment to construction milestones. Access without supply is rationing dressed as a right.

No merit, no solidarity, no future: they gave you a master's and kept the apartments.

They promised that effort and solidarity would be rewarded. They aren't. And the dominant ideologies manage scarcity instead of unlocking opportunity.

The pact, as it was sold to us, went something like this: here, take this tool — a degree, a master's, English, an internship — and do something with it. Make sure what you do gives back to the system that paid for the tool. A whole generation took the tools and walked out into the world to find the doors locked. The few that opened demanded what they didn't have, paid wages that wouldn't cover rent within commuting distance of the job, or both. The tool, it turns out, didn't open doors.

Then, a few years later, came the reproach: why haven't you done more with the tool we gave you? Where is your career? Where are your apartments? Where are your kids? I didn't have a tool like yours. How do you explain that having a tool is not the same as having the chance to use one?

The reproach comes in two flavors. The merit version says you didn't try hard enough, weren't flexible enough, aimed too high. The solidarity version says the older generation worked their lives off and now it's your turn to fund their pensions and healthcare, and you should be grateful for the country you inherited. Both demands assume the same thing: that there's something on the other side. That contribution gets paid back, by the market or by the state. That's the exact thing that has gone missing.

The data tells a clean story. Between 2008 and 2024, the only age group whose real income meaningfully grew is the over-65s. Pensions tracked inflation; wages didn't. The wealth of the two younger generations has collapsed against their predecessors at the same age — essentially because they cannot access housing. Public spending has tilted further toward those who already have, while investment in what could unlock opportunity (housing, energy, transport, research) is flat or falling.

«Sin oportunidad, el mérito se parece a una estafa. Sin oportunidad, la solidaridad se siente como un saqueo.»

"Without opportunity, merit looks like a scam. Without opportunity, solidarity feels like looting."

Most people aged 18–40 want to believe in effort. They want to contribute to a system that takes care of those who need it, including those who came before. They haven't stopped believing in effort. They haven't stopped wanting to contribute. They've stopped seeing the point of doing so. What they need is for that contribution to come with the possibility of getting something back.

And scarcity isn't a fact of nature here — it's a political choice we've made and keep making. The neighbor who blocks the new building. The pensioner who demands his max-pension be indexed to inflation while wages stagnate. The traditional parties who would rather distribute discount vouchers than mobilize land, because the first gives a headline and the second gives a fight with local residents. A wide, implicit coalition of those who already have — house, pension, stability — votes to keep what's theirs. Politicians, not out of malice but out of bias toward what worked for them, oblige.

With opportunity blocked and politics unable to unblock it, two zero-sum ideologies thrive. The right of scarcity: close the door, defend what's ours, the border protects. The left of scarcity: regulate the queue, cap prices, ration what little there is. Both share the conviction that the pie won't grow. Both make sure it doesn't.

The way out — the way to break the deadlock — is to put unlocking opportunity at the center of the political project. Build housing where people want to live. Invest in education and infrastructure that improves what young workers can do. Reform the institutions that gate-keep against newcomers. The argument isn't that pensions or social protection are wrong. It's that they aren't enough on their own, and that without growth that arrives where people start their adult lives, no welfare state will hold.

Growing without arriving.

The growth figures the rest of Europe envies are hard to believe for those starting out inside Spain — because they don't experience them in their daily lives.

Try telling a thirty-year-old who rents in Madrid or Barcelona that the country is doing as well as it appears to be. Spain closed 2025 with GDP up 2.9% on the year, twice the eurozone average. 2026 will likely beat the neighborhood again. But to someone with a double major, two master's degrees and a string of unpaid internships — none quite "in their field" — who watches the apartment they could afford three years ago slip out of reach, the headline figure is hard to credit. Same for the couple in Valencia's metro area who, without university degrees, took the leap to have a first child and are now hesitating about a second. Same for the mother who arrived two years ago to start over.

The growth is real. It's the country attracting people, expanding, moving. Net migration added two million people between 2022 and 2024; 40% of new social-security affiliations come from foreign-born workers. Internal mobility added another 1.75 million municipal moves in 2024. And the demographic bulge from the early-2000s baby boomlet now hits its working-age years. Without all this, the economy would be flat, and so would the cities.

But for those starting, costs and incomes both fail. Take costs. Adult lives start in cities. Average rent rose between end-2019 and end-2025 by 45% in Madrid, 48% in Barcelona, 76% in Valencia, 50% in Málaga. Food has accumulated a 30% increase since end-2021, weighted more heavily toward lower-income households. Take incomes. The minimum wage rose 54% between 2018 and 2024 — a real win at the floor — but the median wage rose only 22%. The share of workers earning up to 125% of the minimum jumped from 8% in 2018 to 23% in 2023, per AIReF. Spain has lifted the floor but not pulled the rest along. The at-risk-of-poverty-or-exclusion rate has barely moved: about 26% today, only seven tenths below where it was a decade ago.

«Hemos elevado el suelo, pero no arrastrado al resto.»

"We've lifted the floor but not pulled the rest along."

Resolving the paradox of an economy that grows but doesn't arrive requires action on both sides. On costs, two priorities dominate: housing supply and food/energy productivity. On housing, the only real long-term answer is more units in the cities where opportunity concentrates — through coordinated zoning reform, public land mobilization, and a sustained construction effort. On the cost-of-living more broadly, structural productivity gains are what move the needle: better food chains, cheaper clean energy, more efficient public transport, transparent and competitive markets.

On incomes, the lever isn't another minimum-wage increase but a productivity shock at the bottom of the wage distribution. That means investment in adult skills and lifelong learning that actually lands; activation of disengaged workers; serious overhaul of the lower tiers of vocational training. It means reducing the regulatory and fiscal frictions that keep small firms small and informal. And it means tying social support to upward mobility, not just to subsistence.

A third lever is the design of public spending itself. Spain spends a growing share of its budget on transfers to those already secure (pensions indexed, accumulated property wealth) and a falling share on the productive infrastructure that helps newcomers get a foothold (housing, transport, education, R&D). Re-balancing this is the harder political fight, but the one that decides whether the next decade of growth will arrive at the lives of those starting out.

Until it does, "Spain grows" will keep being a sentence the country reads in the newspaper rather than feels in the bank account.

Growth without abundance (and where to find it).

If we're growing so much, where's your share — the one you don't see?

Picture a couple, almost 35. They studied something common, moved to Barcelona from another Spanish city or from somewhere in Europe or Latin America, work jobs that aren't quite what they trained for. They earn more than the friends who stayed back home, but it's not obvious they're better off — they rent, the friends bought. The news tells them Spain is growing faster than Germany, France, the eurozone average, and has been for two or three years. They don't need to be economists to see this doesn't square with their lives. They are not better off than two or three years ago. They are clearly not where they thought they'd be when they started adulthood twelve years ago.

They suspect inflation. Of course they do — they've watched it in real time at the supermarket, the gas pump, the rental market. But the official growth figures already discount inflation. So what else?

We grow (mostly) because we are more.

Between 1990 and 2024 Spain's real GDP nearly doubled (+91%). Real GDP per capita grew about half as fast (+52%). The big chunk of aggregate growth comes from more workers — net migration in, and higher participation by women, older workers and others — rather than from each resident producing more. The macro implication: when Spain grows, household income hasn't kept pace, not by a long shot. The political implication is the inevitable question: where's my share?

The rest of the growth goes somewhere else.

Even after population growth, there is per-capita real growth. So why doesn't our hypothetical couple feel it? Has the income gone to the very rich? Not really — the Spanish top 1% sits at the European average and the top 10% slightly below, by World Inequality Lab estimates. But look at average income by age cohort, and a different picture emerges: in two decades, only the over-65 group has meaningfully grown. The 30–44 cohort — where our couple lives — is barely back to where it was before the 2008 crisis. Real spending has fallen for everyone except the over-65s.

Public-sector wages have also held up much better than private-sector ones. The gap between the two has widened, regardless of education level. Pensions and rental income (which feeds into older households' incomes) have outpaced wages. If you stayed in a smaller Spanish city and you happen to be a civil servant, life has been comparatively kind. If you moved to a major metro to chase opportunity and rent — the opposite.

The wrong progressive answer.

Public spending has shifted toward those who already have, while categories that could expand opportunity (transport, infrastructure, R&D, housing) have lost ground. To compensate for the resulting feeling of stagnation, governments have leaned on three policies: legislating income (especially the minimum wage), subsidizing demand for basic goods (cheaper public transport, rental aid, fuel and food tax cuts), and ordering the queue under scarcity (rent controls, price controls). The first has had mixed results. The second, in this framework, is solitaire-cheating: subsidizing prices because incomes won't rise. The third reorganizes crumbs at best, and at worst — see rent control — actively reduces supply and makes entry harder.

«La sensación es la de estar en una cinta de ejercicio: cada vez sube más la velocidad, pero uno siempre está en el mismo sitio.»

"It feels like a treadmill: the speed keeps going up, but you're always in the same place."

Where abundance actually is.

Abundance — meaning growth that arrives in the lives of those starting out — has to come from a deliberate redirection of public action toward creation. Klein and Thompson's Abundance traces the U.S. version of this; the Spanish version, in my reading, comes down to three things. First, unlock the supply that's blocked: apply Garicano's test to every regulation that gates access to housing, energy, transport, computing capacity, research and basic education, and ask whether the rule is helping or hindering. Second, build what the private sector won't — public investment in knowledge and human capital (early-childhood centers, applied research), in the conditions for equitable access to it (housing where people want to live), in efficiency-raising public goods (better local services, climate-resilience infrastructure), and in the protection that lets the system keep working. Third, and above all, shift public spending toward the early stages of life: every euro well spent on 0–3, on academic support, on work–life balance and on research yields a multiple in productivity and equality of opportunity later — and we have a demographic window to do it.

What's really happened with Spanish GDP.

Spain has grown mostly by adding people and hours. Average output per worker has barely moved in three decades.

GDP is the central indicator we use to read an economy. Its importance flows from a consensus: count the goods and services a country produces in a year, translate them into money, and you have a reasonable summary of how things are going. When the count rises, the country is producing more, the state is collecting more, salaries can rise, investment lines up. The summary works — until it stops working as a summary of how households actually live. That's where Spain is now.

Look at the long view. Real Spanish GDP grew dramatically over the last three decades. Decompose it into its parts and a different story emerges. The first part is population: Spain has added roughly 8 million people since 1990, mostly through immigration. The second is hours: the share of the working-age population in employment, plus how many hours each works, has expanded — especially through women's participation and the entry of older workers. The third part — output per hour worked — is what we mean by productivity proper. And here Spain's record is, charitably, modest. Output per hour worked has grown, but at a pace well behind the European leaders, and far behind what the headline GDP figure suggests.

Why does this distinction matter? Because the source of growth determines whether it can be felt. Growing through population and hours is real growth — more output, more tax revenue, more activity. But it doesn't automatically translate into rising household income, because each worker isn't producing more on average. If you split a slightly larger pie among more, faster-tiring eaters, each plate doesn't get fuller. That is broadly what has happened. Spain has become a bigger economy without becoming a more productive one per worker.

«España ha crecido sobre todo sumando gente y horas; la aportación media por trabajador ha avanzado poco en tres décadas.»

"Spain has grown mostly by adding people and hours; average output per worker has barely moved in three decades."

The diagnosis has practical consequences. First, on the public conversation: when politicians and commentators wave the headline GDP growth at Spaniards who don't feel it, they are not technically wrong, but they are systematically misleading. The two numbers — total GDP and per-worker output — point in different directions, and only one tracks the lived experience of those starting their working life. Second, on policy: the response to weak per-worker output is not the same as the response to weak total growth. It requires investment in education, reskilling, machinery, R&D, and the institutional and infrastructure conditions that let firms scale. Subsidizing demand or capping prices does not move productivity per worker. Pension increases indexed to inflation don't either.

Third, on fairness. The benefits of population-driven, hours-driven growth do not automatically reach those who add the population or the hours. Migrants, young adults, mothers returning to the labor market — these are the protagonists of the headline figure. They are also, disproportionately, the protagonists of housing scarcity, of low wages relative to costs, of the precarious entry into adult life. The summary statistic flatters Spain at a moment when its productive structure is creaking under the very people generating that statistic.

A productivity decade — for the lower half of the wage distribution especially — is the only thing that converts headline growth into lived improvement. Without it, GDP just keeps making a sentence that fewer and fewer people recognize as their own.

Households without roofs: escaping Spain's housing supply scarcity.

A long-form article — adapted from the book — on why Spain's housing crisis is, fundamentally, a supply problem in a country whose successful cities are pulling demand inward.

When you publish a book and people invite you to talk about it, what you do, in practice, is rewrite it. The most fertile prompt for that rewriting is a good critique. The best one I received was: why focus the book on Spain when affordable-housing scarcity is a problem in so many cities globally? I could have started the book there, with Edward Glaeser and his argument that dense cities drive innovation, productivity and social mobility because they make face-to-face exchange possible. I could have followed Klein and Thompson's Abundance, which builds on Glaeser to argue that, in advanced economies, ideas account for a growing share of value-added — and so where ideas are produced, exchanged and tested matters more than ever.

The Spanish version of this argument runs through the work of De la Roca and Puga, who tracked nearly two million Spaniards over years and found that for each logarithmic point of urban size there is an immediate wage premium. The premium grows over time and persists even after workers leave the city — because of agglomeration economies (sharing infrastructure and labor markets), selection (more able workers concentrate in cities), and learning (dense networks raise the value of human capital acquired in urban environments). Cities leave a footprint on lives.

But for that footprint to form, you need access. You need a roof near where the opportunity concentrates. And in Spain — like in many cities globally — that roof has become structurally hard to find.

Demand keeps rising.

INE projects 3.7 million new households in Spain. Behind that figure are two forces: continuing migration (642,000 net entries in 2023, the highest in the EU) and shrinking household size (from 2.5 to 2.32 average people per household; one third of all households will be single-person). And the new households cluster: Madrid will gain 576,000, Valencia 238,000, Alicante 247,000, Málaga 211,000, Murcia 164,000. At the other end, Zamora barely 1,300, Palencia 2,100, Soria under 5,000, Teruel 9,600.

On top of that, cities attract not just residents but visitors and seasonal users. Spain hit a pre-pandemic record in 2023 with over 85 million international tourists, and the Ministry projects 94 million in 2024. Officially-registered tourist housing has grown around 75% between 2016 and 2024. Seasonal rental, in parallel, has scaled — in Barcelona it went from 2.1% of listings in 2019 to 14.4% in 2023; in Madrid, from 3.3% to nearly 10%. Investment returns reflect this: the Bank of Spain's average annual return on housing was 10.5% combining rent and capital gains between 2015 and 2022, with rent alone contributing 5.5%. A market of scarcity, rewarding those already in it.

Supply keeps not responding.

The Bank of Spain estimated in 2023 that the accumulated housing deficit in 2022–2023 was around 375,000 units, with another 225,000 expected over 2024–2025. The headline that dominated coverage for a year and stuck in collective memory: roughly 600,000 units short by 2025. CaixaBank Research and FUNCAS, with different methods, arrived at similar magnitudes. Meanwhile, only 128,000 new homes were started in 2024, by construction permits — far below what's needed to close the gap. The 2025 trend is barely 11% higher.

The gap shows up downstream. Youth emancipation is falling: in 2015, 58% of those aged 18–34 lived with parents or depended on parental income; in 2024, 67.2%. With 6.13 million people in that age range, the gap to the European average (under 50%) suggests roughly 1.65 million additional young Spaniards living with parents — about 800,000 households that don't yet exist.

Why the market doesn't respond.

BBVA Research's 2024 report on the topic is, in my reading, the best summary of the supply-side blockages. Land where people want to live is locked under zoning regimes written for different decades. Construction productivity is low and has stagnated. Cost structure has tilted hostile — materials, labor, financing, regulatory friction. Risk and uncertainty drive professional capital toward second-hand stock and short-term tourist rental rather than new construction. The market signal is loud; the response is muted because the channel is clogged.

The state, which historically picked up some of the slack in northern European countries, doesn't here: Spain's public housing stock sits around 2.5% of the total, among Europe's lowest. The 2008–2014 austerity gutted public construction capacity. Local governments, who hold zoning and land, face an asymmetric incentive: existing residents vote, future residents don't.

What unblocking would look like.

The book's prescription, which I summarized above, applies here. The order of magnitude needed — three million homes over a decade, concentrated where demand is — is feasible if four things move together: zoning reform with national criteria sitting above local veto; large-scale mobilization of public land combined with density bonuses on private land; serious public construction with skin-in-the-game public–private partnerships; and a sequencing of demand-side support that doesn't undermine supply expansion. None of this is novel. None of it is happening at the scale the diagnosis demands.

«Tener un techo cerca de donde están las oportunidades es la condición necesaria para poder contribuir a y beneficiarse de la economía urbana.»

"Having a roof close to where opportunity concentrates is the necessary condition for being able to contribute to and benefit from the urban economy."

Build or decay.

The conversations we're already having about present and future policy involve building actively: infrastructure as the platform for opportunity and growth.

In 2012 I had emigrated. Many of us had: Spain's net migration balance flipped because a credit-and-real-estate bubble had burst. What stayed in the collective imagination was that bricks had eaten a generation and a half's expectations. Then came the Plan E stimulus, which spent on infrastructure of dubious value. The bad pre- and post-crisis decisions chained into the European debt crisis, in which we starred alongside our neighbors.

The decade-long trauma was an understandable counterweight to a decade of brick-based growth. In 2012, foreign journalists came to Spain for the white-elephant tour. When they passed through Valencia — my home, the epicenter of the crisis — I would take them here:

A 45-meter concrete viewing tower in the middle of a €24 million roundabout, closed within months of inauguration. When Spaniards — and outsiders — think of public infrastructure here, this is what they see. Reasonably so. But we now need to think beyond it. The lessons learned from that decade can serve us in the context we are entering, where the material, the tangible, is taking on new prominence in our most consequential decisions.

A recent recap: a major blackout where both the immediate operator response and the network's lack of mechanisms for an increasingly renewable-heavy mix played a role; a climate emergency demanding more than regulation, with disasters like the Dana flooding requiring a more prepared environment; defense investment that already has 2% of GDP as a floor and is looking at 3% as a ceiling; a slow but inexorable shift in the housing debate toward the need for more supply; the question of what to do with our supply chains in a more uncertain trade environment; and the disruptions to energy and other supplies that began before Russia's invasion of Ukraine. Add it up: after years of mostly symbolic or cultural battles, the material is reclaiming its space.

To accommodate that, I propose starting from an apparent paradox that isn't quite one: how do we align infrastructure investment with an economy whose growth source is increasingly the value of ideas, knowledge, and human capital? The proposal is to use that itself as the measuring stick. Worthwhile material investments are those that meet at least one of four criteria:

  1. They strengthen knowledge and human capital. Examples: 0–3 early childhood centers; specialized and applied research centers; better-integrated vocational training systems.
  2. They enable equitable access to it. Example: affordable housing where people want to come to study or work.
  3. They build on those contributions, raising efficiency, productivity or public goods through ideas-based innovation. Examples: alert and adaptation systems for climate emergencies; next-generation vaccines; AI for public services.
  4. They protect us so we can keep contributing. Examples: joint EU defense capabilities; protection of critical connectivity infrastructure.

The frame, borrowing from Fernando Caballero's Madrid DF, is infrastructure as platform — extending it across scales and sectors. Platforms on which more can be built. You can do little on a useless viewing tower or on a housing block built in the middle of nowhere. You can do a lot leaning on housing built where people want to live (and can't), on new early-childhood centers, on better-equipped existing schools and universities, on a more robust energy system less dependent on partners we should not trust, on transport networks that are faster, more efficient and failure-tolerant.

«Hacer cosas para que otras personas puedan hacer más.»

"Building things so that other people can build more."

It is not at all clear that the €80 billion Spain received in pandemic recovery funds met this approach. With 95% already committed, redirection is difficult — but reviewing it systematically would still teach us something. Circumstantial evidence that Europe's NextGen execution has not, on the whole, built growth platforms is the very existence of the Draghi report on European competitiveness. One reading of that report is a call to achieve enough economic scale to take advantage of the continent's potential. Part of it is "let it happen" — unblock private investment and action. Another part, equally relevant, is about doing. Building, at the EU, national or local level, so that others can build more.

There's a political opening here for progressive politics, which Klein and Thompson identified as Abundance and which Yglesias, Smith and others have been making in the U.S. for some time. But to take it, progressive discourse has to free itself from five reflexes that today feel too native to it. First, zero-sum thinking — the assumption that progress means rationing the little there is, with degrowth as the harder, less defensible version of the same idea. Second, an over-reliance on regulation as the main lever — the paradigmatic Spanish example being the obsession with capping rents in cities where the question we should be asking is whether we are building enough. Third, the inertia of welfare-state spending on items that have grown obsolete while crowding out areas that would do more for opportunity — pensions indexed by decree versus public investment in 0–3 childcare. Fourth, a discourse weighted toward symbolic and culture-war issues that yields little political return and distracts from the material agenda. And fifth, underestimating that voters attach value to a sense of order and protection — not as an end in itself, but as the floor on which everything else can be built.

And if we don't do this? Decline, I suspect. The trouble with decline is that it doesn't arrive as a shock, the way the bubble did — leaving an unmistakable lesson behind it. Decline is what happens when nothing happens. It is, by construction, hard to compare to the world that could have been. But the comparison is the one we are obliged to keep making — because between building a viewing tower in a roundabout and sharing out scarcity, there is a third path we are taking too long to walk.

Ending poverty: beyond Spain's current Minimum Vital Income.

A productivity shock at the bottom of the wage distribution would help us more than we think.

In 2021 Spain made a significant statement of intent against poverty, approving the most consequential social policy of the decade: the Minimum Vital Income (IMV). Four years on, the IMV reaches roughly 400,000 households. And yet about a quarter of the Spanish population still lives in households at risk of poverty or social exclusion. The gap between intention and outcome reflects the structural limits of the design — and points to where the next move has to come from.

To understand the gap, it helps to break the at-risk-of-poverty-or-exclusion indicator into its three components: monetary poverty (income below 60% of the median), severe material deprivation, and low work intensity (a working-age household employed less than 20% of possible hours). The IMV is designed as a basic safety net pegged to monetary poverty, and AIReF reports that most eligible households don't even apply for it. The persistence of this take-up problem should make those of us who advocate for cash transfers think hard about the design of the policy itself. But it also leaves a more uncomfortable conclusion on the table: Spain doesn't have one of the highest poverty rates among rich countries because its IMV is too small. In a market economy, the heavy lifting against poverty is done by labor income — and Spain's poverty rate is fully consistent with its very high unemployment.

The data backs this up. Reducing unemployment has been the main lever pulling Spaniards out of poverty: between 2014 and 2024, the share of people in low-work-intensity households at risk of poverty or social exclusion fell from 13.4% to 6.3%. That is what works. It is also where the IMV's design quietly cuts against itself: AIReF's most recent report finds that receiving the IMV reduces the probability of working by up to about 5 percentage points for those receiving the most generous benefits, and that this effect persists even after the 2022 reform introduced employment incentives intended to avoid a euro-for-euro clawback between wages and benefit.

So the IMV needs rethinking — not discarding. A unified transfer is still useful, especially in inflationary episodes. The data also show that material-deprivation poverty has risen over the past decade, and there the IMV has been a missed opportunity: we have preferred specific subsidies (transport, rent, energy) and tax breaks rather than channeling support through the Income, which would have been more effective, more efficient and fairer.

The most concerning trend, though, is the rise in purely monetary poverty toward 13%. Households are escaping the other categories but still earning incomes below 60% of the national median. In-work poverty has been stuck at around 11% since 2008 — Spain is the third EU country with the most working poor — even as poverty among retirees has fallen sharply (from about 20% to 13%). Behind that lies a labor market with very unequal hours and unstable jobs at the bottom of the distribution, plus very low wages there.

Trying to fix this from the demand side alone — by repeatedly raising the minimum wage by decree — has hit visible limits. The Bank of Spain estimated that the 2019 minimum-wage increase raised the long-term probability of job loss by about 3.2 percentage points and reduced full-time-equivalent hours by around 4 points. The ISEAK Foundation finds gentler effects, but still negative on work intensity (about −0.84 pp after a year). And if we redo the poverty/unemployment correlation with GDP per capita instead, Spain sits about 5 percentage points above the poverty rate its income would predict — a sign that the issue is distributional inefficiency in how growth reaches the bottom, not just the floor of transfers.

«Nos vendría bien un 'shock' de productividad en la parte inferior de la distribución salarial.»

"A productivity shock at the bottom of the wage distribution would do us a lot of good."

What we need, then, is a productivity shock at the bottom of the wage distribution: pairing demand-side policy with supply-side measures that lift earning capacity from below. The usual suspects apply (firm size, production model), but I'd put particular weight on one lever that also bites on structural unemployment: investing in human capital for people at the bottom of the distribution. Concretely: only 1 in every 40 euros of NextGen funds went to vocational training. Spain has a dual-VET system that hasn't really taken off, despite evidence — Bentolila and co-authors — that it raises days worked by 25% and cumulative income by 42% in the first two years. Vocational training is rarely framed as an anti-poverty tool. Few things move long-run upward mobility for the lower half of the income distribution as effectively.

Progress = infrastructure (on El 47).

Why a film about a bus to a peripheral neighborhood of Barcelona is the most important reminder of what all this is — or should be — about.

I left the cinema moved. Not just by extraordinary performances, an excellent script, perfect cinematography, well-arranged music. I'd seen a film that worked as a forceful reminder of the kind of public, collective decision that has the largest possible effect on progress through improving people's lives.

No spoilers: El 47 is about the bus with that number, and the battle of Torre Baró to bring public transport up to that hill. What is "up there"? Roughly this — a photo I took in Can Baró, another barrio that was once Barcelona's periphery — a slope of self-built homes erected by families newly arrived from Extremadura and Andalusia in the 1950s. About 10 km uphill from Plaça Catalunya. Residents whose jobs were down at the bottom of the hill didn't walk all of it, but they walked a lot of it: the descent to the nearest bus stop. Most services we now take for granted barely reached them.

Without going into the many complexities the neighborhood has lived through (worth reading on their own), I want to use that image to underline what the film reminded me of: if, among everything we can do collectively, we want to choose what makes the biggest difference in people's lives, start with what improves their material conditions. And the way to solve that, with the most consensus, is to build things.

Building things is also useful from a political-economy standpoint, because it dissolves part of the winners-versus-losers dilemma. You don't take a bus route away to put another in. You don't move a school from one place to another. You add a bus, you build a school. The other part of the dilemma it doesn't solve, of course: budgets are limited, you have to choose what to build. But that's the kind of political-economy dilemma I think we want to be having.

Take a current example. I don't want public debate to spend all its energy on whether rent controls are better or worse than rental subsidies. Some debate about them is necessary because they're in force, and we have to evaluate their effects. But shouldn't we be spending more time on where to create new accessible housing supply, and how to do it? On whether limited budgetary resources move toward rehabilitation or social rental, in this place or that. At the end of the decade, what stays is the house — not a measure that lasted a few months or years.

Another example: Lucas Gortazar and I have argued that the Spanish education system is likely to enjoy a more comfortable per-student investment ratio in the years ahead as demographics empty out classrooms. But other classrooms are pressed by problems we haven't solved: segregation, repetition, failure, weaknesses in math and science, schedules that protect or help neither students nor parents. Shouldn't we be talking much more about which of these problems we will direct that surplus toward?

«Infraestructura son cosas palpables que soportan el progreso.»

"Infrastructure is palpable things that support progress."

And note: infrastructure is not only cement, buses, train lines. It's also hiring people, training them, paying them properly (and demanding accordingly) so they deliver the services we consider most necessary. It's books, computers, healthy food in schools. Infrastructure is palpable things that support progress.

Two doubts this might be raising. First, doing things has costs beyond the budgetary — pollution, CO2 — and we know this much better than fifty years ago. The answer is to price those costs in (we're slowly doing this, which leads to better-targeted decisions) and to invest in decarbonization infrastructure: home retrofits, low-emission heating, basic research for clean energy. Eliminating emissions can also make us more efficient, as the Draghi report stresses — also in the public sphere.

Second: there isn't that much left to build. Torre Baró got its bus, the Madrid and Barcelona peripheries got theirs. We built more present health and education systems, closer to universal. To which I'd respond: maybe there isn't that much left from your vantage point, looking back from a relatively comfortable position. I'm not at all sure that someone living in one of the millions of Spanish homes with thermal-insulation problems would tell you there isn't much to do. Or someone in a neighborhood whose health center is overwhelmed, with unworkable doctor-to-patient ratios. There is plenty of ambition still required, in very specific places: those with lower income, more idle population, fewer health professionals.

One of my most vivid childhood memories is the day I rode the tram for the first time. We were inaugurating Line 4 in Valencia. It ran as far as Doctor Lluch, a ten-minute walk from my house in Cabanyal. Spring of 1994. I was eight. We had buses that worked, more or less — but this was something else, a tram that connected my neighborhood with the north of the city: where my pool was, where my closest friends lived, where the university and my first job-in-the-field would later be. What I remember clearly is the feeling that something new had been added to the city, and that it would be there tomorrow. That's what it means, in concrete, to build a platform.
